# Section 1: About the Question

## Lesson 0 — Introduction

This course focuses on the **"Write About a Photo"** question type on the Duolingo English Test. It is one of the hardest questions on the test because you must describe a photo **in only 60 seconds**.

**Course structure:**

- **Section 1** — The question type: what you have to do, time limits, and how to score well.
- **Section 2** — Making your answer: structure, grammar, and vocabulary.
- **Section 3** — Practice and sample answers.

**Why this matters:** This question contributes to both your **Production** score and your **Literacy** score.

---

## Lesson 1 — Different Writing Questions

### 7 Key Points You Must Know

1. **Time limit:** You have **only 60 seconds**. The clock starts the moment the photo appears — no preparation time.
2. **Frequency:** You will get this question **3 times** in the test (sometimes back-to-back).
3. **Minimum requirement:** You must write at least **1 full sentence** (or you get zero). But writing 2–3 sentences is strongly recommended.
4. **Full sentences only:** You cannot just label the image with keywords. You need proper sentences with capital letters, punctuation, commas, and correct spelling.
5. **Subscores affected:** Contributes to your **Production** and **Literacy** subscores.
6. **Photo variety:** Photos can include people, vehicles, nature, fireworks, festivals, buildings, etc. At least one photo will likely have people in it.
7. **Every photo has something clear to describe** — never think there is nothing to write about.

### Test-Taking Strategies

| Strategy | Details |
|----------|---------|
| Use all 60 seconds | No bonus for finishing early. Use the full time to write and check. |
| Write more than required | The minimum is 1 sentence, but 2–3 is much better for your score. |
| Corrections are free | You won't be penalized for mistakes until you click "Next." |
| Don't watch the timer | Looking at the clock in the top left distracts you. |
| Leave ~10 seconds to review | Quickly check your answer before submitting. |

### General Writing Tips

- Use **correct spelling, grammar, punctuation, and capitalization**.
- Both **American and British spelling** are accepted for writing questions.
- **Vary your sentence structure and word choice** — diverse and sophisticated vocabulary and grammar = better score.

---

## Lesson 2 — The Scoring Criteria

Duolingo uses the same criteria for all speaking and writing questions. Here is the **simplified version** of what you are assessed on:

### The 5 Scoring Areas

| # | Criterion | What It Means | How to Score High |
|---|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Grammatical Accuracy** | Writing error-free sentences | Avoid spelling/grammar mistakes; proofread in your last 10 seconds |
| 2 | **Grammatical Complexity** | Using a variety of grammar structures | Mix simple and complex sentences (relative clauses, subordinate clauses, etc.) |
| 3 | **Lexical Sophistication** | Using specific, precise, and advanced words | Don't only use basic words — include some advanced vocabulary |
| 4 | **Lexical Diversity** | Using a variety of words, not repeating | Avoid repeating the same words; use synonyms |
| 5 | **Task Relevance** | Actually describing the photo | Stay on topic — describe what you see |
| 6 | **Length** | How much you write | Write 2–3 sentences (aim for 35–50 words) |
| 7 | **Fluency** | Cohesion + spelling + punctuation | Link your ideas smoothly; use connecting words |

> **Key takeaway:** You need a balance of accuracy AND complexity. Don't just write simple sentences, but also don't write complex sentences with mistakes. Tailor your studies to your weaknesses.

## Lesson 3 — How to Structure Your Answer

### Step 1: Identify the Main Subject(s) IMMEDIATELY

As soon as the photo appears, quickly find the **main subject** (and secondary subjects). This is what you will write about.

### The 3-Sentence Structure

| Sentence | Purpose | Example |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **Sentence 1** | General summary of the main subject | *"This is a picture of a man giving a presentation to a group of people."* |
| **Sentence 2** | More detail: **who is doing what** OR **what is where** | *"He is holding something in his right hand and talking to three men sitting directly in front of him."* |
| **Sentence 3** | Speculate / make a guess | *"Since he is dressed casually, this might not be an important presentation."* |

> **This structure is flexible, not strict.** If you can only write 2 sentences, that's OK. If you can type quickly, aim for 3.

### Example — Photo WITH People (man giving presentation)

**Answer 1 (48 words):**
*"This is a picture of a man giving a presentation to a group of people. He is holding something in his right hand and talking to three men who are sitting directly in front of him. Since he is dressed casually, this might not be an important presentation."*

**Answer 2 (45 words):**
*"This is an image of a young man standing and talking to three people. He is wearing a blue shirt and white shorts and he is standing in front of a large monitor. It seems that he is practicing giving a presentation to his friends."*

### Example — Photo WITHOUT People (camper van by a lake)

**Answer 1 (48 words):**
*"This is a photo of a blue camper van that is parked by a beautiful lake. The lake is in front of the camper van and there are hills in the background. As the weather looks perfect, there must be a lot of people swimming in the lake."*

**Answer 2 (45 words):**
*"This is a photo of an old van parked near a large blue lake. The van is facing the beautiful lake and there is a small road to the right of it. The driver must have stopped to enjoy the amazing view of the lake."*

## Lesson 4 — Writing the First Sentence

### What to Write

Your first sentence should be a **general overview / summary** of what the main subject is doing or what is happening. Imagine you are describing the picture to someone who cannot see it.

### Introductory Phrases (Memorize at least 3!)

| Phrase | Example |
|--------|---------|
| There is / There are... | *There is a man and a woman sitting on a bench.* |
| In this photograph, there is/are... | *In this photograph, there is a helicopter taking off.* |
| This picture shows... | *This picture shows a tractor in a field.* |
| This image shows... | *This image shows two colleagues working together.* |
| This is a photo of... | *This is a photo of fireworks over a city.* |
| This picture depicts... | *This picture depicts a man sitting on his motorbike.* |

> **Tips:**
> - Use different introductory phrases for each of the 3 photos (don't repeat!).
> - The words *photograph, picture, image, photo* all mean the same — vary them.
> - Practice typing these phrases quickly.

### Ways to Add Detail to Sentence 1

| Technique | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Articles/Determiners** (a, some, three, a lot of, many, several) | *"...some people," "...three cars," "...a lot of cows"* |
| **Relative clauses** (who, which, that) | *"...a man **who is** cooking," "...a car **that is** in a field"* |
| **Reduced relative clauses** (remove who/that + is) | *"...a man cooking," "...a car in a field"* |
| **"With" for two objects** | *"...a man who is cooking **with** his son"* |
| **Prepositions** | *"...a car in a field **next to** some trees"* |

> Adding detail = **grammatical complexity** = higher score!

### Full Examples of Sentence 1

- *"This is an image of a woman serving food at a party."*
- *"This picture shows a tractor in a field with a man sitting on top."*
- *"This image depicts a beach with many small boats in the water nearby."*

## Lesson 5 — Common Mistakes in the First Sentence

### Mistake 1: "There is" vs. "There are" confusion

**Rule:** When using "There is / There are," the verb agrees with the **FIRST subject**.

| Sentence | Correct? | Why |
|----------|----------|-----|
| ~~There are a man and a woman sitting on the bench.~~ | ❌ | First subject "a man" is singular |
| There **is** a man and a woman sitting on the bench. | ✅ | "a man" = singular → use "is" |
| A man and a woman **are** sitting on the bench. | ✅ | Subjects come first and are plural → use "are" |
| There **is** a teacher and students. | ✅ | "a teacher" = singular → use "is" |
| There **is** a car and a bus. | ✅ | "a car" = singular → use "is" |

### Mistake 2: Wrong Articles and Determiners

Always use the correct article/determiner before nouns:

| Correct Usage | Example |
|---------------|---------|
| a + singular noun | *This photo shows **a** man.* |
| an + vowel sound | *This is an image of **an** igloo.* |
| some + plural | *In this picture, there are **some** men.* |
| a group of + plural | *The image shows **a group of** women.* |
| three (spell out numbers) | *There are **three** dogs.* |
| several + plural | *There are **several** pieces of equipment.* |

> **Tip:** Always spell out numbers (write "three" not "3").

### Mistake 3: Irregular Plural Nouns

English has many irregular plurals. Watch out for these common ones:

| Singular | Plural | Common Error |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| child | **children** | ~~childs~~ |
| sheep | **sheep** | ~~sheeps~~ |
| person | **people** | ~~persons~~ |
| woman | **women** | ~~womans~~ |
| man | **men** | ~~mans~~ |
| traffic | **traffic** (uncountable) | ~~traffics~~ |
| furniture | **furniture** (uncountable) | ~~furnitures~~ |
| trash | **trash** (uncountable) | ~~trashs~~ |
| book | **books** | (regular) |

## Lesson 7 — Who Is Doing What?

### The Grammar: Present Continuous Tense

To describe **actions happening in the photo**, use the **present continuous tense**:

**Structure:** Subject + is/are + verb-ing

| Example | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| *The man **is walking** down the street.* | Singular subject → "is" |
| *The students **are taking** an exam.* | Plural subject → "are" |

### Describing Two Actions

Use **and**, **while**, or **but** to combine two actions in one sentence.

**Same subject (no comma needed):**
- *"The woman is wearing a pink top **and** carrying two plates of food."*
- No need to repeat "she is" — just use "and" + verb-ing.

**Different subjects (comma needed):**
- *"The woman is serving food to the guests at the party**,** and the men are talking to each other."*
- Use a comma before "and" / "but" when subjects differ.

**Simultaneous actions (use "while"):**
- *"The woman in Navy is pointing a pen at the laptop **while** the lady in red is reading the information on the screen."*

### Grammar Tips for Comma Usage

| Situation | Comma? | Example |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Same subject, two actions | **No comma** | *She is cooking and listening to music.* |
| Different subjects, two actions | **Comma before "and/but"** | *She is cooking**,** and he is reading.* |

### Pro Tip: When You Don't Know the Action Verb

If you can't think of the action word, **describe what they are wearing** instead:
- *"The woman is wearing a pink top."*
- *"I am wearing a navy sweater."*

This is a simple but effective fallback!

### Describing Emotions (Present Simple)

Sometimes there's no clear action. Use the **present simple** for states/emotions:
- *"The couple **are** sitting next to each other and **are** unhappy."*
- *"The man **looks** tired."*
- *"She **seems** excited."*

## Lesson 8 — What Is Where?

### Prepositional Phrases for Location

This is one of the most powerful tools for the second sentence. It helps you describe **where things are positioned** in the photo.

### Essential Prepositional Phrases

**Basic Positions:**

| Phrase | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| on top of / above / at the top | Higher position |
| under / beneath / underneath / below | Lower position |
| in the center / in the middle | Central position |
| in the corner | At a corner |
| to the left (of) / to the right (of) | Side positions |

**Depth & Distance:**

| Phrase | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| in the background | Behind the main subject, far away |
| in the foreground | In front, closest to the viewer |
| in the distance | Very far away |
| in front of | Directly before something |
| behind | Directly after/behind something |

**Advanced/Precise Phrases:**

| Phrase | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| directly opposite | Facing each other |
| exactly in the middle of | Precisely centered |
| roughly in the middle of | Approximately centered |
| in close proximity to | Very near |
| just above | Slightly higher than |
| near to | Close by |
| on the left-hand side / right-hand side | Specific side |
| in the upper left-hand corner | Top-left area |
| in the lower right-hand corner | Bottom-right area |

### Examples with Full Sentences

**Lighthouse photo:**
- *"The man is standing to the right of the lighthouse and looking up."*
- *"Above the lighthouse in the sky, there are beautiful clouds."*
- *"The ocean is in the background."*
- *"There is green grass in front of the lighthouse and an ocean behind it."*

**Combined Sentence 1 + Sentence 2:**
- *"This is a photo of a beautiful red and white lighthouse with a man standing next to it. The man is standing to the right of the lighthouse and looking up at the sky."*

**Man on a bike:**
- *"The man is in the middle of the photo and he is looking at the mountains in the background."*
- *"In the foreground, there is a man sitting on a bike and in front of him there is a river and a mountain range."*

**Woman serving food:**
- *"The woman is exactly in the middle of the photo and there is a group of men behind her."*
- *"There are a group of men chatting in the background of the image and a lady serving dishes in the foreground."*

## Lesson 9 — Speculating

### What Is Speculating?

Making a **guess** about what is happening, what happened before, why something is happening, or how someone feels. This is great for your **third sentence** (but can go anywhere).

### Speculation Structures — Present

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| might / may / must / could + verb | *They **might be** discussing something important.* |
| I think / I guess / I suppose... | *I **think** the woman is a waitress at the party.* |
| ...seems like... | *She **seems like** a waitress.* |
| ...seems to be... | *She **seems to be** a waitress.* |
| ...looks like... | *It **looks like** they are working on a project.* |
| ...appears to be... | *The woman **appears to be** a waitress.* |
| probably | *He is **probably** taking a break.* |

### Speculation Structures — Past

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| must / may / might / could + have + past participle | *They **must have** broken up.* |
| | *He **might have** been riding for a long time.* |
| | *She **could have** arrived early.* |

### Full Answer Examples with Speculation (highlighted)

**Woman shopping in a supermarket (36 words):**
*"This is a picture of a young woman shopping in a supermarket. She is looking at the large pile of pineapples to her left and pushing a shopping cart. She **might be** buying fruit for a party."*

**Man writing in a notepad (41 words):**
*"This is an image of a man writing in a notepad with a yellow pen. There is a laptop directly in front of him, which he is **probably** using for work. He **appears to be** taking detailed notes for his job."*

### Practice Speculation Sentences

**Man on a motorbike:**
- *"He **might be** taking a break from riding his bike."*
- *"He **must have** been riding for a long time."*
- *"He is **probably** waiting for his friend to come and join him."*

**Beach with boats:**
- *"This beach **must be** somewhere tropical, like Thailand."*
- *"I **suppose** those boats are used for fishing."*
- *"The weather **seems to be** really hot and humid."*

**Two women working:**
- *"They **may be** preparing an important presentation for work."*
- *"It **looks like** they are working in a big office building."*
- *"I **guess** that the woman in Navy is giving feedback to the woman in red."*

> **Remember:** Your guesses don't need to be exciting — they just need to be grammatically correct and related to the photo.

## Lesson 10 — Adjectives & Adjective Order

### Why Use Adjectives?

Adjectives are the **easiest way** to add more detail to your answer. Use them in any sentence (1, 2, or 3) whenever you mention a noun.

**Without adjectives:** *"The woman is reading a book."*
**With adjectives:** *"The **young** woman is reading a **fascinating** book."*

### Adjective Categories

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| 1. Opinion | amazing, fantastic, brilliant, terrible, awful, beautiful, wonderful, exciting |
| 2. Size | small, big, huge, large, tiny, tall, short |
| 3. Physical Quality | thick, thin, smooth, rough, soft |
| 4. Age | young, old, ancient, modern, new |
| 5. Shape | square, round, circular, rectangular |
| 6. Color | black, white, blue, red, green, pink, yellow |
| 7. Origin | Chinese, English, American, Japanese |
| 8. Material | wooden, plastic, metal, glass, stone |
| 9. Purpose (-ing words) | cooking (equipment), cleaning (tool), sleeping (bag) |

### Adjective Order Rule

When using multiple adjectives, follow this order:

**Determiner → Opinion → Size → Physical Quality → Shape → Color → Origin → Material → Purpose → NOUN**

> **Simple tip for 2 adjectives:** **Opinion FIRST, Fact SECOND**
> - Opinion: beautiful, amazing, exciting, wonderful
> - Fact: big, small, green, blue, metal, wooden, old, young

### Examples with Correct Adjective Order

| Sentence | Order Used |
|----------|-----------|
| *This is a picture of a **big silver** truck.* | size + material |
| *This is a photo of a **tall young** man.* | size + age |
| *There are several **delicious soft** cakes in the window.* | opinion + quality |
| *There is a **small old metal** boat near the **calm blue** sea.* | size + age + material / quality + color |

### Comma Rules for Adjectives

- **Different categories** → **No comma**: *a big silver truck* ✅
- **Same category** → Use **"and"**: *an **interesting and unique** old car* (both opinion) ✅
- *There is a **wonderful red and white** lighthouse.* ("red" and "white" are both colors → use "and")

## Lesson 11 — Adverbs

### Adverbs of Manner

Adverbs of manner describe **how** something is happening. They add detail to verbs/actions.

**Formation:** Most are made by adding **-ly** to adjectives.

| Adjective | Adverb |
|-----------|--------|
| quick | quick**ly** |
| slow | slow**ly** |
| bad | bad**ly** |
| happy | happi**ly** |
| easy | easi**ly** |
| peaceful | peaceful**ly** |
| skillful | skillful**ly** |

**Irregular adverbs:**

| Adjective | Adverb |
|-----------|--------|
| good | **well** |
| fast | **fast** (no change) |
| hard | **hard** (no change) |
| straight | **straight** (no change) |

### Adverb Placement (2 positions)

| Position | Rule | Example |
|----------|------|---------|
| After the **main verb** | Place directly after "is/are" | *The woman **is peacefully** reading a fascinating book.* |
| After the **object** | Place at the end of the clause | *The woman is reading a fascinating book **peacefully**.* |

> **Common mistake:** Do NOT place the adverb between the gerund (-ing verb) and the object.
> - ❌ *The woman is reading peacefully a book.* (WRONG)
> - ✅ *The woman is peacefully reading a book.* (CORRECT — after main verb "is")
> - ✅ *The woman is reading a book peacefully.* (CORRECT — after object "book")

> **Simple rule:** If you're unsure where the main verb is, just put the adverb **at the end** after the object. That's always safe.

### Practice Examples

| Photo | Sentence with Adverb |
|-------|---------------------|
| Couple laughing | *This is a picture of a couple laughing **loudly**.* |
| Sports car | *This is a photo of a sports car driving **fast/quickly/dangerously**.* |
| Family playing | *In this image, there is a family playing together **happily/joyfully/cheerfully**.* |
| Colleagues reading | *This photo depicts two colleagues reading from the screen **thoroughly/seriously/thoughtfully**.* |

## 10 Connecting Words to Raise Your Score

These words improve **fluency and cohesion** — two scoring criteria!

### 1. For instance
- Synonym for "for example" (slightly rarer → more points)
- *"There are a lot of beautiful places in California. **For instance**, Lake Tahoe is gorgeous year round."*
- Does NOT require a new sentence.

### 2. On one hand & on the other hand
- Shows contrast elegantly.
- *"**On one hand**, I like going to the beach for vacation. **On the other hand**, it can be very expensive."*
- "On one hand" can be implied (just use "on the other hand").

### 3. Therefore
- Formal synonym for "so" (shows cause → effect).
- *"He did not study for the exam. **Therefore**, he did not pass."*
- Requires a **new sentence**.

### 4. Firstly, secondly, thirdly, finally
- Excellent for structuring longer responses (Writing Sample / Speaking Sample).
- *"I prefer team sports for several reasons. **Firstly**, it is fun... **Secondly**, team sports teach us... **Finally**, I find team sports more interesting."*

### 5. Next
- Simple way to move from one idea to another in sequence.
- *"On Saturday, I woke up and went for a run. **Next**, I had brunch with my family."*

### 6. In other words
- Introduces a clarification or simpler explanation.
- *"The project has experienced significant challenges. **In other words**, it will not be completed on time."*

### 7. Furthermore
- Synonym for "moreover" — adds more information that builds on a previous idea.
- *"The young woman is highly intelligent. **Furthermore**, she works very hard."*
- **Be careful:** Don't use it more than once in the same response. Don't use it to connect unrelated ideas.
- ❌ *I like chocolate ice cream. Furthermore, my best friend likes strawberry.* (WRONG — unrelated ideas)
- ✅ *I like chocolate ice cream. However, my best friend likes strawberry.* (CORRECT)

### 8. Moreover
- Direct synonym for "furthermore." Same rules apply.
- You can alternate between "moreover" and "furthermore" for variety.

### 9. Additionally
- Close synonym of "furthermore" and "moreover." Also works as a synonym for "also."
- *"Exercise will make you healthy. **Additionally**, it will make you feel great."*

### 10. Similarly
- Emphasizes when two ideas are **similar**.
- *"He decided to join the army. **Similarly**, his sister became a police officer."*
- *"New York is wonderful because of its restaurants. **Similarly**, Paris also has delicious food."*
