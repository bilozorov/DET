## Lesson 0: Course Overview

This course focuses on the **Write About the Topic** question type on the Duolingo English Test.

- **Task:** Read a prompt and write a paragraph response
- **Time limit:** 5 minutes
- **Minimum word count:** 50 words (aim for 70–110)
- **Frequency:** Appears **once** on the test
- **Contributes to:** Production and Literacy subscores

The course covers three areas: understanding the question, writing your answer, and practicing with sample responses.

## Lesson 1: Scoring Criteria Breakdown

You are assessed on **7 official criteria** (simplified into **5 key areas**):

### 1. Grammar
| What they look for | How to score well |
|---|---|
| Grammatical accuracy | Write mostly error-free sentences |
| Grammatical complexity | Use a **mix** of simple and complex structures |
| Variety of structures | Don't repeat the same sentence pattern — combine compound and complex sentences |

**Bad example (all simple sentences):**
> I live in an apartment. I really like my apartment. It's modern. It's conveniently located.

**Improved version (mixed structures):**
> I live in an apartment which I really like. It's modern and conveniently located. In fact, it only takes me 10 minutes to walk to school. Since the apartment is a bit small, I'd like to find a bigger one in the future.

### 2. Vocabulary
| What they look for | How to score well |
|---|---|
| Wide range (lexical diversity) | Use synonyms — don't repeat the same words |
| Sophistication | Replace general words (nice, good) with specific ones |
| Topic-related vocabulary | Show you know words connected to the topic |
| Formal/academic style | **No contractions** (use "do not" not "don't"), **no slang** |

**Synonym examples:**
- students → learners, freshmen
- classes → lessons, lectures

### 3. Task Relevance
- **Fully answer all parts of the prompt** — read it carefully
- Provide **supporting ideas** (reasons, examples, facts)
- Use **topic-related vocabulary**

### 4. Length
- Minimum: 50 words (writing fewer = score of zero)
- **Aim for 70–110 words** — longer responses with good grammar score higher
- You will NOT get a better score for finishing early

### 5. Fluency
- Use **linking/transition phrases** to connect ideas smoothly
- Ensure correct **spelling and punctuation**
- Linking phrases = smoothness = fluency

> **Key takeaway:** Treat all 5 criteria as equally important. Tailor your practice to cover all of them.

## Lesson 2: The Writing Questions on the DET

### Key Facts About "Write About the Topic"
- **5 minutes** — timer starts immediately (no preparation time)
- Appears **once** on the test
- Must write **at least 50 words** (but try for more)
- Write **one paragraph**, not a full essay
- Answer is auto-submitted when time runs out
- Both **American and British spelling** accepted
- **No spell checker** is provided — practice without one

### Test-Taking Strategies (from Duolingo's website)
1. **Use all 5 minutes** — no bonus for finishing early; use extra time to proofread
2. **Write more than 50 words** if you can keep sentences accurate
3. **You are not penalized for mistakes until you click "Next"** — make corrections before submitting
4. **Don't watch the timer constantly** — it distracts you
5. **Leave time to check your response** at the end

### General Writing Tips
- Correct spelling, grammar, punctuation, and capitalization
- **Vary sentence structure** — mix simple and complex
- **Vary word choice** — use synonyms, avoid repeating the same words
- Use diverse, sophisticated vocabulary alongside simpler words

## Lesson 3: Three Types of Writing Prompts

### Overview Table

| Type | What it means | Do you give an opinion? | Key signal words |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Recount** | Tell a story about your experiences | No (share experiences) | "What have you learned/done…?" |
| **Describe** | Discuss a situation (pros/cons) | No (just describe) | "Discuss…", "Explain…" |
| **Argument** | Discuss AND give your opinion | **Yes** (agree/disagree) | "Do you agree or disagree?" |

### Recount
- Talk about **your own past experiences**
- Pick **one topic** and develop it with examples
- Example prompt: *"Films can tell us a lot about the country where they were made. What have you learned about another country from watching its movies?"*

**Idea tips:** Pick one country you know from films. Write about 2 things you learned (e.g., food customs, language, music).

### Describe
- Discuss **both sides** of a situation (pros and cons)
- You do **not** need an opinion (though you can add one in the conclusion)
- Example prompt: *"In general, people are living longer today. Discuss some of the implications of this phenomenon."*

**Idea tips:** Write 1 positive and 1 negative implication with examples (e.g., positive = grandparents spend more time with grandchildren; negative = higher taxes).

### Argument
- State a **clear position** (agree or disagree)
- Argue **why** your position is correct with reasons and examples
- This is the **hardest** prompt type
- Example prompt: *"People are never satisfied with what they have. They always want more. Do you agree or disagree?"*

**Idea tips:** Pick one side. Write 2 reasons why you agree/disagree. Structure: "I agree/disagree because [reason 1] and [reason 2]."

## Lesson 4: Organizing Your Answer

### Paragraph Structure (use for ALL prompt types)

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│  1. TOPIC SENTENCE                  │  ← 1 sentence (introduction)
├─────────────────────────────────────┤
│  2. SUPPORTING IDEAS                │  ← The bulk of your paragraph
│     • Idea 1 + example/fact/reason  │     (aim for 2 ideas)
│     • Idea 2 + example/fact/reason  │
├─────────────────────────────────────┤
│  3. CONCLUDING SENTENCE             │  ← 1 sentence (restate topic sentence)
└─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### What each part does:
- **Topic sentence:** First sentence. Tells the reader what the paragraph is about. For argument prompts, state your opinion clearly.
- **Supporting ideas:** Explain and develop the topic sentence using reasons, facts, examples, or descriptions.
- **Concluding sentence:** Signals the end. Restates the topic sentence in different words. Begins with a linking phrase.

> **Important:** Unlike IELTS/TOEFL, you only write **one paragraph** (not a full essay). Keep the topic sentence to just 1–2 sentences.

## Lesson 5: Writing a Topic Sentence

### Key Rules
1. **Topic + Central Point** — be specific, not too general
2. Keep it **short** — 1 sentence (sometimes 2)
3. **Don't list your ideas** in the topic sentence — save those for supporting ideas
4. **Don't include too much detail** — leave room for the rest of the paragraph
5. For **argument** prompts: state your opinion clearly

### Bad vs. Good Topic Sentences

❌ **Too general:** "Exercise has benefits."

✅ **Specific:** "Exercise has many health benefits for people of all ages."

❌ **Lists ideas:** "Exercise has many health benefits such as lowering blood pressure, reducing heart problems, increasing stamina, and improving mental health."

✅ **Just the central point:** "Exercise has many health benefits for people of all ages."

### Grammar Tenses for Topic Sentences

**For Describe & Argument prompts:**

| Tense | When to use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present simple | General statements/facts | "Nowadays, fewer people eat a healthy diet." |
| Present continuous | Things changing right now | "It is clear that video games are becoming more popular." |
| Present perfect | Past changes affecting present | "In the past 10 years, the price of food has risen sharply." |

**For Recount prompts:**

| Tense | When to use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Past simple | Finished actions | "I went on many trips when I was young, but my favorite was to France." |
| Present perfect | Experiences (no specific date) | "I have learned so many things about India from Bollywood movies." |
| Present simple | General facts about yourself | "I am not the kind of person who gets angry easily." |

### Opinion Phrases (for Argument prompts)
- In my opinion, …
- I feel that …
- It is my view that …
- I agree/disagree …
- I strongly agree/disagree …
- [Something] should/must …
- Many people claim that …
- It is often said that …

### Example Topic Sentences

| Prompt | Example topic sentence |
|---|---|
| All students should wear uniforms. Agree or disagree? | "School uniforms should be mandatory for all high school students for a number of important reasons." |
| People are living longer. Discuss implications. | "Life expectancy is increasing all around the world, and this has both positive and negative implications." |
| What have you learned from movies? | "There are many things that I have learned from watching Korean films." |

## Lesson 6: Writing Supporting Ideas

### Structure for Each Supporting Idea

```
Supporting idea (general statement)
    ↓
Back it up with: example / fact / reason / description
```

### Full Example

**Question:** Fast food should be banned from schools. Agree or disagree?

**Topic sentence:** "I agree that children should be banned from eating fast food at school for several important reasons."

**Supporting idea 1:** "First, children should not be allowed to eat fast food because it is detrimental to their health."
→ **Example:** "For instance, fast food burgers are packed with fat, sugar, salt and preservatives, which make them extremely unhealthy, especially for children."

**Supporting idea 2:** "In addition, schools should stop serving fast food because it is very expensive."
→ **Fact:** "It is a well-known fact that fresh food from a supermarket is significantly cheaper."

### Transition Phrases — MEMORIZE THESE

**Sequencing (same viewpoint):**
- First / Firstly / First of all / First and foremost
- Second / Secondly / Third / Thirdly
- In addition / Furthermore / Moreover
- Another reason is… / Another point to consider is…

**Contrasting (opposite viewpoint):**
- However, …
- On the other hand, …
- That being said, …
- Alternatively, …
- Although [X], …

**Examples and Facts:**
- For example, … / For instance, …
- Such as …
- To illustrate this point, …
- It is well known that … / It is a well-known fact that …
- It has been proven that …
- Many studies have shown that …

> **Tip:** Memorize these phrases and practice typing them quickly. On test day, you should be able to type them without thinking.

## Lesson 7: Writing a Concluding Sentence

### Key Points
- The **easiest** part of the paragraph
- Simply **restate the topic sentence in different words**
- Always begin with a **linking phrase**
- Does NOT need to be complicated

### Concluding Linking Phrases (academic/formal)
- In conclusion, …
- To conclude, …
- To sum up, …
- In sum, …
- Overall, …
- On the whole, …
- All things considered, …
- Given these points, …
- All in all, …

### Example

**Topic sentence:** "I agree that children should be banned from eating fast food at school for several important reasons."

**Concluding sentence:** "To conclude, for the reasons presented above, I believe it is vital that schools stop serving fast food to their students."

→ Notice: same idea, different wording. Add a linking phrase at the start.

### More Examples

| Topic sentence idea | Concluding sentence |
|---|---|
| Learned a lot from Korean films | "To sum up, my experience watching Korean movies has taught me a lot about the culture and language." |
| Fast food should be banned | "To conclude, for the reasons presented above, it is vital that schools ban fast food." |
| Pros and cons of living longer | "All things considered, there are a number of positive and negative implications of people living longer." |

## Lesson 8: Grammar 1 — Compound Sentences

### What is a Compound Sentence?
Two **independent clauses** linked by a **coordinating conjunction**.

```
[Independent clause 1] + , + [coordinating conjunction] + [Independent clause 2]
```

An independent clause = has at least a **subject + verb** and can stand alone.

### FANBOYS — The 7 Coordinating Conjunctions

| Letter | Word | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **F** | For | reason/cause |
| **A** | And | addition |
| **N** | Nor | negative addition |
| **B** | But | contrast |
| **O** | Or | alternative |
| **Y** | Yet | contrast (stronger) |
| **S** | So | result |

### Comma Rules

**Rule 1: Use a comma when FANBOYS links two independent clauses.**
- ✅ "She wore a beautiful dress, **but** she didn't wear anything else."
  (second clause has its own subject + verb → comma needed)
- ❌ No comma when combining nouns/adjectives: "I think taxes are both good **and** bad."

**Rule 2: No comma when combining nouns, verbs, or adjectives.**
- "After high school, you can get a job **or** go to university." (combining verbs — no comma)

**Rule 3: Same subject = you can omit the repeated subject (and the comma).**
- With repeat: "I was tired, **but** I went to work early."
- Without repeat: "I was tired **but** went to work early."

### Examples
- "The Internet is very important, **and** it is widely used everywhere."
- "Snack food is good sometimes, **but/yet** it can be addictive, **so** we must be careful."

> You can link **3+ ideas** with coordinating conjunctions in one sentence.

## Lesson 9: Grammar 2 — Complex Sentences (Subordinating Conjunctions)

### What is a Complex Sentence?
Two clauses linked by a **subordinating conjunction**. Slightly harder than compound sentences but very important for grammatical complexity.

### Subordinating Conjunctions by Category

| Category | Conjunctions |
|---|---|
| **Time** | after, as, since, until, while, once |
| **Contrast** | although, even though, though, whereas, while, rather than |
| **Reason** | so that, in order to, as, since, because |
| **Condition** | if, even if, provided that, unless, whether |

### Comma Rules for Complex Sentences

**Subordinating conjunction at the START → use a comma:**
> "**Although** many students like playing sports**,** other students prefer to make art."

**Subordinating conjunction in the MIDDLE → NO comma:**
> "Many students like playing sports **whereas** other students prefer making art."

### Examples by Category

**Contrast:**
> "Although many people dislike paying taxes, it is important to remember that taxpayer money pays for basic services."

**Reason:**
> "Online games have advanced rapidly because many children find these games irresistible."

**Condition:**
> "If we didn't pay our taxes, the government would be unable to invest in the future."

**Contrast (even though):**
> "Even though many people argue that we should pay less taxes, I believe taxation is vital in order to maintain a high standard of living."

### Practice Examples
- "School is not all about studying **since** there are a lot of clubs and fun activities on campus."
- "**Even though** cars cause a lot of pollution, they are essential to our lives."
- "Most children will participate in sports **provided that** they are encouraged."
- "The environment will improve **once** the number of cars decreases."

## Lesson 10: How to Generate Ideas

### 6 Essential Tips

1. **Get used to thinking about different topics** — read the news (ideally in English) to learn topic vocabulary
2. **Preview common topics:**
   - **Describe/Argument:** health, mental health, work, school/education, public transport, technology, culture, cities/towns
   - **Recount:** films, books, music, art, inspirational people, family life, emotions (surprise, fear), vacations, education
3. **Build vocabulary lists** for each topic — find words online or from reading articles
4. **Practice with the course questions** and compare your answers to the sample answers
5. **Form your opinions in advance** — it's easier to write about what you actually think than to invent an opinion on the spot
6. **Practice writing every day** — keep a diary, respond to practice prompts, and always time yourself (5 minutes)

> **Remember:** Your ideas don't need to be unique or special. Simple, clear ideas with good examples are perfectly fine.

## Lesson 11: Recount — Example Questions & Answers

### Example 1
**Prompt:** Think about your childhood. Were your parents or guardians strict or relaxed?

**Sample answer (104 words):**

> **Topic sentence:** Fortunately, my parents were very strict with me when I was a child.
>
> **Supporting ideas:** The main reason they were strict and protective of me was because I was an only child. For instance, they would never allow me to stay out late since I would have to walk home alone. Additionally, they rarely allowed me to watch TV as they believed it was a bad influence on me. Instead, they encouraged me to read books and make art.
>
> **Concluding sentence:** Overall, even though my parents were controlling, I do not feel upset about it because I know they were just trying to do the best for me.

### Example 2
**Prompt:** Films can tell us a lot about the country where they were made. What have you learned about another country from watching its movies?

**Sample answer (107 words):**

> **Topic sentence:** There are many things that I have learned from watching Korean films.
>
> **Supporting ideas:** Firstly, the most interesting thing I have learned is about Korean eating customs. For example, when dining at a restaurant, Koreans usually order a large amount of food that is shared among all guests rather than each person ordering their own dish. Also, through films, I have improved my Korean language skills. Although I am studying Korean at college, watching Korean movies has taught me a lot of spoken expressions that do not appear in textbooks.
>
> **Concluding sentence:** To sum up, I have learned a great deal about Korean culture and language from watching its films.

### Key Patterns in Recount Answers
- Uses **past tenses** (past simple, present perfect)
- Talks about **personal experience** with specific examples
- 2 supporting ideas with backing detail
- Clear topic sentence + concluding sentence that restates it

## Lesson 12: Describe — Example Questions & Answers

### Example 1
**Prompt:** What are the benefits of reading? Include specific examples in your answer.

**Sample answer (100 words):**

> **Topic sentence:** Reading is probably one of the most beneficial activities you can do.
>
> **Supporting ideas:** The first reason why reading is so beneficial is because it increases your knowledge. Filling your mind with new facts, new information and new ideas will make you better at conversations, as you will always have something interesting to talk about. Furthermore, it is a well-known fact that reading frequently improves your vocabulary. The more you read, the more words you will be exposed to, which will improve your ability to articulate your thoughts more effectively.
>
> **Concluding sentence:** Overall, reading on a consistent basis will benefit your life in many different ways.

### Example 2
**Prompt:** In general, people are living longer today. Discuss some of the implications of this phenomenon.

**Sample answer (104 words):**

> **Topic sentence:** Life expectancy is increasing all around the world, and this has both positive and negative implications.
>
> **Supporting ideas:** One negative implication is the burden it has on the taxpayer. Since more and more people are living longer and having longer retirements, there is a shortage of funds available for essentials such as pensions and health care. On the other hand, there are social benefits of this phenomenon. For instance, grandparents have more time to spend with their grandchildren, which can help build a stronger sense of community and encourage family values.
>
> **Concluding sentence:** All things considered, there are a number of positive and negative implications of people living longer.

### Key Patterns in Describe Answers
- Uses **present tenses** mostly
- Discusses **both sides** (positive and negative)
- Uses contrast transitions ("On the other hand")
- Does NOT state a personal opinion (or only briefly in conclusion)

## Lesson 13: Argument — Example Questions & Answers

### Example 1
**Prompt:** Many people believe that schools should be banned from serving fast food to their students. Do you agree or disagree? Provide reasons.

**Sample answer (97 words):**

> **Topic sentence:** I agree that children should be banned from eating fast food at school for two important reasons.
>
> **Supporting ideas:** First, fast food is detrimental to children's health. For instance, fast food is often packed with fat, sugar, salt and preservatives, which makes them extremely unhealthy for children. In addition, schools should stop selling fast food because it is very expensive. It is a well-known fact that fresh food from a supermarket is significantly cheaper than premade fast food.
>
> **Concluding sentence:** To conclude, for the reasons presented above, I believe it is vital that schools stop serving fast food to their students.

### Example 2
**Prompt:** A university plans to develop a new research center in your country. Some people want a center for business research, while others want a center for research in agriculture. Which do you recommend and why?

**Sample answer (94 words):**

> **Topic sentence:** While both proposals would be beneficial to my country, I recommend investing in a business research center for the following reasons.
>
> **Supporting ideas:** First and foremost, since my country is currently suffering from an economic downturn, investing in a business research center would likely provide more solutions to these economic problems than an agricultural research center. Furthermore, businesses tend to create more jobs than agriculture. As unemployment is currently high, creating jobs is a top priority.
>
> **Concluding sentence:** Given these points, I believe investing in a business research center would yield the most positive results for my country.

### Key Patterns in Argument Answers
- **States opinion clearly** in topic sentence ("I agree/disagree", "I recommend")
- Gives **specific reasons** why the opinion is correct
- Uses phrases like "First and foremost", "Furthermore"
- Concluding sentence **restates the opinion**

## Quick Reference: Complete Paragraph Template

```
[TOPIC SENTENCE]
State the topic + central point. For arguments: state your opinion.

[SUPPORTING IDEA 1]
First/Firstly/First and foremost, [general idea].
For example/For instance/It is a well-known fact that [specific detail].

[SUPPORTING IDEA 2]
In addition/Furthermore/Moreover/On the other hand, [general idea].
[Backing example, fact, or reason].

[CONCLUDING SENTENCE]
In conclusion/To sum up/All things considered, [restate topic sentence in different words].
```

**Target: 70–110 words in 5 minutes.**
